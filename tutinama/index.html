The Tutinama:
A Digital Art History Student Project

The Tutinama is a Persian set of tales told by a pet parrot to convince a wife not to cheat on her husband. The tales were a translation of a text originally composed in Sanskrit and dating back to ancient India. In the 16th century, the Mughal Indian emperor Akbar ordered an illustrated copy be made for his enjoyment, a copy that now resides in the Cleveland Museum of Art (CMA 1962.279).

Below, you will find a collection of student digital multimedia work on select folios from the Cleveland Tutinama. Tap on the images below to play a short video about individual folios. To dig deeper, tap on the titles to open English translations of the episodes depicted. The folio numbers below will open the record in the Cleveland Museum of Art's online digital collections.

Who?

In the spring term of 2019, students enrolled in Dr. Hussein Keshani's course ARTH 375 - Encountering India: The Age of the Mughals at UBC Okanagan in Kelowna, Canada, were assigned the task of creating a multimedia web animation. They were asked to discuss an episode from the Tutinama using the Cleveland Museum of Art's manuscript 1962.279. Most had no knowledge of Mughal art history or web programming!

The Tutinama (The Book of the Parrot)

The Sanskrit work Shukasaptati (Seventy Tales of the Parrot) was authored and performed as part of the katha storytelling tradition. Clues to its date can be found in the fact its title is mentioned in an 1160 AD Jain text composed under the Indonesian Srivijayan empire, though some tales appear in older Indian texts dating as far back as 200 BC. With the rise of the Delhi Sultanates, it was translated into ornate Persian as Jawahir al-asmar (Jewels of Stories) by â€˜Imad ibn Muhammad Tajri a courtier of Ala al-Din Khalji (r. 1290-1316). Another simpler and shorter Persian version with 52 stories was produced over a decade later in 1329-30 by Ziya al-Din Nakhshabi (d. around 751/1350-51), which was called the Tutinama. Nakhshabi was a Persian speaking Central Asian Turk, who was trained in religious sciences, as well as Chishti mysticism, and had settled in India. Under the Mughal ruler Akbar, an illustrated copy of Nakhshabi's text was commissioned around 1560 or 1570. The text was subsequently condensed and further simplified by Muhammad Qadiri in 1793 (George Small, The Tota Kahani: Or, Tales of a Parrot, p. vi) to 35 tales, which were translated into English by the British Orientalist scholar Francis Gladwin in 1801. The tales have also been translated/adapted into Bengali, Hindi, Urdu (Saiyid Haidar Baksh), Turkish, German, Czech, Russian and French.

The Tutinama follows a frame narrative structure, that is, stories are told within a story. In the master narrative, a parrot tells his mistress, Khojisteh, a series of allegorical stories over the course of several nights in order to dissuade her from slipping away to meet a prospective lover while her husband, Miemun, is away on an extended trip.

CMA 1962.279

The Cleveland Tutinama manuscript consists of 341 bifolia of which 218 folia contain paintings rendered with opaque watercolours. The text is Persian written with Arabic script in a naskh style. Additional folia, likely from the same manuscript, are held in other museums like the Freer Sackler in Washington D.C. (F1991.8) and the San Diego Museum of Art (1990.273).

Other illustrated manuscripts, or portions of them, can be found at the Chester Beatty Library (Mughal manuscript no. 21), the University of Kentucky Libraries Special Collections (UK Special Collections Arabic Script Codex #10), the Virginia Museum of Fine Arts (68.8.47), the Victoria and Albert Museum (IS.40-1966), Delhi's National Museum, the Bharat Kala Bhavan and The David Collection (Inv. no. 3/1999).

The Cleveland manuscript is considered a key example in Akbar's early patronage of illustrated manuscripts, reflecting a layering of the techniques of transplanted Persian painters Mir Saiyyid Ali and Abd al-Samad and Indian Jain, Sultanate and Early Rajput manuscript painting traditions. Twelve artists' names appear within, including that of Basvana (Basawan), Dasvant and Tara. Curiously, over half of the manuscript shows signs of overpainting, raising questions about shifts in painting style preference.

Learning Goals

The goals included improving abilities to observe visual works closely, read primary sources closely, and conduct focused secondary source research. In terms of communication, the goal was to enhance students abilities to communicate specialist material to a general audience in verbal, textual and visual ways. With regard to digital literacy, the goal was to familiarize students with basic web development and multimedia production.

Technical

The project uses HTML page and Javascript (with the GSAP library) templates developed by Keshani for the Evolving the Botanic Garden research project, an example of research being applied to the classroom. Students were trained in class on how to modify the templates in order to complete their project. The projects are actually compact web animations with soundtracks and not videos.
